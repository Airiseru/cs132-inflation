InPHlation

A time series analysis and trend analysis on the inflation rate of the Philippines from 1995 to 2023

Discover More

Overview

Did you know that in 2012, bangus (or milkfish) was P76 less expensive per kilo than in 2022 (Statista, 2023)? As prices continue to rise over time, people are finding it more and more difficult to afford things that they used to be able to afford. Will the general public be able to keep up with the increase in prices moving forward? What exactly are the factors that affect the apparent increase in prices? How worried should we be given that inflation is being viewed as an “urgent national concern?”

Rationale

Problem

That is why we decided to focus on inflation rate. We wanted to see the trend of inflation rate and determine the possible factors or events that ultimately affect the ongoing worry of being able to afford basic needs and wants.

Specifically, the lack of awareness and understanding about inflation and its effects causes a negative perception on the topic. This leaves policymakers, businesses, and the general public, especially those in lower income brackets, vulnerable and unprepared to deal with the rapid changes in inflation and the effects that come with it.

Solution

We want to use data science to gather facts about inflation, analyze its trends, and possibly predict inflation rates to increase awareness, change our perception of inflation, and possibly take steps to minimize its effects on our lives.

Background

The title of “the Sick Man of Asia” has once again been given to the Philippines

In recent years, the Philippines has experienced a plethora of problems regarding inflation, as the average yearly inflation has soared to 6.6%, which makes the Philippines the country with the highest inflation rate among all Southeast Asian nations ever since the pandemic

However despite the increase of inflation and overall price of goods, majority of the workers have yet to receive an increase of wage. In fact, majority of the full-time workers receive an average monthly basic pay of PHP 13,000. That is why, in the Philippines, inflation is mostly perceived in a negative light due to the concern that it has adverse effects on the people, especially for those with a limited income

Research Question 2: How much does the rate of NCR differ from or affect the rate of areas outside NCR (AONCR)?

Null Hypothesis

There is a correlation (significant relationship) between the inflation rates of the "All Items" commodity group of NCR and AONCR.

Alternative Hypothesis

There is no correlation (insignificant relationship) between the inflation rates of the "All Items" commodity group of NCR and AONCR.

Before getting to the answer, we must first check the data. To start, we check the general trend of the data.

Notice that in both plots, there is a surprisingly downward trend, as indicated by the regression line. Although this may not be indicative of what is actually happening, this is a good thing because it tells us that there is a linear trend which satisfies the first part of cross correlation. An Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Test was then performed to check whether the data is stationary or not. As seen below, the p-value of NCR was not less than 0.05, which indicates that the data for all items in NCR is not stationary.

Since one of the series is not stationary, then we applied detrending. For consistency, detrending was applied to both of the series. This was done to isolate the fluctuation in the data from the trend.

With that, we are finally ready to find the cross correlation coefficients. Plotting the coefficients and lags with respect to the detrended value, we get the following:

To check the significance of the coefficients found, the Granger Causality Test was performed. Performing the test after 12 months and 100 months, it was found that the p-values were 0.03 and 0.2163 respectively.
Note that for this case, when p < 0.05, it indicates that Granger-causes Y. Granger-causes simply indicate that knowing the value of X at a certain lag is useful for predicting the value of Y at a later time, indicating that they are correlated.

Machine Learning Process

For our machine learning model, we decided to perform the Box-Jenkins methodology on the data.
The Box-Jenkins methodology is a set of steps that helps identify the parameters for the Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average Model, or ARIMA(p, d, q) model, or its seasonal counterpart, SARIMAX(p, d, q)(P, D, Q)s. This involves a couple of steps:

Identification

The first stage of the Box-Jenkins methodology is to identify different aspects of the data such as whether it’s stationary, its seasonality, and differencing order.

Identification of Stationarity

Using the .info() method on each dataset (PHL, NCR, AONCR), we noticed that there were no null values and the type in each column is consistent or accurate with what is expected. This is great as we don’t have to worry about how to deal with them.

Among all the groups in the significant commodity groups (All Items, Food and Non-Alcoholic Beverages, Alcoholic Beverages and Tobacco, Housing and other Fuels, Health, and Transport), only two commodities failed the stationarity test: Housing, Water, Electricity, Gas, and other Fuels, and Health.

ACF and PACF Analysis

For each commodity, the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) and Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF) were graphed. These graphs will help identify the ARIMA models to be used for each commodity group. For the stationary commodities, the correlation on the ACF graphs (left graph) gradually lowered as lags increased, and on the PACF graphs (right graph), the values had an abrupt cutoff after the first lag. This implies that for those commodities, an ARIMA(p, 0, 0) model can be used.

For the non-stationary commodities, differencing was performed in order to achieve stationarity. We can see that removing the trend from Health and Housing gave us ACF and PACF graphs that cut off after the first lag, suggesting that we need to use both AR and MA components of ARIMA.

Seasonality Check

The commodity groups were then tested for seasonality. Using the additive seasonal decomposition function in stattools, we found that all of the commodity groups had a yearly seasonality component as the graphs had distinct, repeating patterns. You can check out the graphs here.

Differencing Order

To obtain stationarity for the commodity groups that were not stationary, namely Health and Housing [etc..], a differencing order of 1 was used to achieve stationarity. This also means that for our ARIMA(p, d, q) model, the differencing order d will be equal to 1. For all other commodity groups, which were stationary, a differencing order of 0 was used.

Parameter Estimation

For the stationary commodity groups (All Items, Food and Non-Alcoholic Beverages, Alcoholic Beverages and Tobacco, Transport), the parameter p was chosen through the ACF plots. For all of these models, it was observed that the term with the highest correlation at any lag is at the first lag, meaning all of these commodity groups would use the AR(1) model for forecasting.

For the non-stationary commodity groups (Housing, Water, Electricity, Gas, and other Fuels, and Health), the pmdarima library was used. This library tries to give the best model based on a range of values. From our test, it was found that the best model for Housing would be SARIMAX(3, 1, 0)(2, 1, 0)12 and SARIMAX(3, 1, 1)(2, 1, 0)12 for Health.

Diagnosis

After creating the models and getting errors, we tried to make sense of them by analyzing what the values mean and how they impact the overall prediction of the inflation rate.

Commodity Group RMSE r² Ljung-Box

It can be observed that the AR(1) models with seasonality have a relatively larger Root Mean Squared Error. This means that the difference between the predicted and the actual values for the model was relatively larger. This may be due to the behavior of the AR(1) model. Having fewer terms in the characteristic polynomial, the behavior is limited to be close to linear.

These models follow the average trend of the data better but are not good at predicting the spikes and dips in the data. They do, however, have a high value returned from the Ljung-Box test, indicating a high level of randomness in the model predictions, so each predicted data point is less dependent on previous data, which is good for a predictive model.

As for Health and Transport, where different parameters were used, we can see that we obtained a smaller Root Mean Squared Error. This means that the distance between predicted and actual values is relatively smaller than the other models. Due to the behavior of their ARIMA models, the characteristic polynomial had more terms, which allowed for more fluctuations in the prediction. This allowed them to make more accurate predictions, especially since the original data also had many fluctuations.

The p-values obtained from their Ljung-Box tests, however, are low, especially for Health, which was less than 0.05. This means that for Health, the predicted data points need to be sufficiently random, and that residuals might affect the prediction of new data points due to high correlation with previous data points.

Results and Conclusions

So… what exactly did we find and what does this tell us?

Research Question 1

To test the hypothesis, a simple linear regression test based on the OLS model was used to determine the general slope of the line of inflation when graphed against time.

For most of the results, the regression yielded a positive coefficient, except for the two commodity groups, “Alcoholic Beverages and Tobacco”, and “Recreation, Sport, and Culture”.

A positive coefficient simply indicates that the coefficients have an increasing trend with respect to time while a negative coefficient indicates a decreasing trend.

This simply indicates that while the commodity groups show a trend (increase or decrease) over time, it is not that significant. That is, there is not much of a linear trend between the commodity groups and time.

Research Question 2

The graph simply indicates at time lag 0, there is a significantly high correlation between the two. But at different time lags, the correlation fluctuates around 0, suggesting that there is no significant correlation between the two time series when lag is applied.

The Granger Causality simply backs this up. Since the p-value fluctuates depending on the lag, it essentially means that while there is a slight correlation between each variable, one cannot be used to predict the other.

This means that we can use NCR data to predict the inflation rate of AONCR and vice versa, but only up to a certain point. This indicates the fluctuating nature of inflation and how hard it is to predict.

Machine Learning

When predicting the inflation rate of the groups in the significant commodity groups, most models had a generally high root mean squared error (RMSE) value, indicating that the model was not able to predict the inflation rate well.

Based on the actual inflation rate vs predicted inflation rate graphs (can be found in the Colab), we see that the predictions were generally incredibly far off from the actual inflation rates.

This tells us that the inflation rate, by nature, is highly unpredictable and is very hard to model. This is due to the unpredictable nature of humans. Since inflation rate is dependent on many real-world factors such as demand, policies, and the general economy, it can be very hard to measure and forecast.
